مَاذَا تَقُولُونَ إِن قَالَ النَّبِيُّ لَكُمَّ
مَاذَا فَعَلْتُم وَأَنتُم آخِرُ الْأُمَمِ
بِأهْلِ بَيْتِي وَأَنْصَارِي وَمَحْرَمَتِي
أبو الأسود الدؤلي
ماذا تقولون إن قال النبي لكم
What Will You Say
✕
Translation
"What will you say when the Prophet turns to you and asks: / What did you do — you, the last nation? / What did you do with my family, my supporters, the ones I held sacred — / some of them captive, some dead, their blood still wet?"
The Depth
Abu al-Aswad composed this as a response to the political turmoil following the assassination of Uthman and the Battle of the Camel. The raw accusation — 'what did you do with my family' — breaks formal qasida convention by speaking directly to the listener as if they are personally guilty. The repetition of 'what did you do' creates an interrogative rhythm that builds unbearable moral pressure.
The Author
Abu al-Aswad al-Du'ali (d. 688) was a companion of Ali ibn Abi Talib, governor of Basra, and one of the earliest Arabic grammarians — traditionally credited with inventing the system of diacritical marks (tashkil) that make Arabic readable.
